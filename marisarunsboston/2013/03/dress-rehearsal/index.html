Dress rehearsal

March 25, 2013 · by Marisa · in Marathon Coalition, Marathon team

On the bus to Hopkinton; our loyal fan club; 21 miles; 21 miles complete! and a particularly inspiring post 21-mile fortune

On Saturday, like hundreds of other runners, I was dropped off at the Boston Marathon starting line in Hopkinton and began my longest and most important run yet. After missing the 19 miler and weeks of knee and IT band pain, I was more than a little nervous about the very crucial 21 miles ahead.

Prior to boarding the busses to Hopkinton, Marathon Coalition coach Rick Muhr imparted some very useful advice on how to navigate the course–run the first 10K of mostly descending roads conservatively, find your rhythm between miles 10-15, take it easier on the Newton Hills from 16-21, and let your adrenaline + everything you’ve got left carry you to the finish line. Easy enough, right?

Pacing has been an issue for me all training season, starting every long run with miles a good 30 seconds faster than what I end with–a pattern that I really couldn’t afford to follow on my initial downhill miles if I wanted to make it to the last few uphill.

Fortunately, I was in excellent company from the starting line through Wellesley; running with my HCF teammate Sue not only kept my pace incredibly even, but made the time and miles fly by. From the beginning we agreed that neither of us were out to break any records, but that there were no hard feelings if she or I wanted to speed up, slow down, or put our headphones on.

At times, especially on the early descents when other runners were zipping by, I thought I wanted to speed up, but thanks to my even keeled running partner, I didn’t. And rather than thinking about how many miles I’d gone, or how many more were left, I enjoyed them.

Marathon Strong trainer Jason was at the 10K point in Framingham to cheer us on, and again four miles down the road in Natick along with HCF Executive Director Lauren, who jumped right in to run with us. A few quick miles later I was surprised to see that we were already making our way into Wellesley, the marathon’s halfway point, and a part of the course that Sue and I knew well by now.

Being able to recognize the course was both relieving, knowing that we were getting close, and intimidating, knowing the difficult stretches ahead. At mile 14, my legs were certainly feeling the effects of all those downhills and I knew it was time to slow down and say goodbye to Sue. Lauren kindly stayed with me just past the dreariest part of the course, mile 16, over Route 128, parting by reminding me just how little having 4+ miles left was when I had already made it so far.

I can’t say the rest of the way was a breeze or that I powered through every hill ahead–it wasn’t, and I walked up nearly every hill between miles 17-21. But, I can say that I felt pretty good, and, finally, ecstatic when I was met with cheers from 3 familiar faces at the day’s finish line, atop Heartbreak Hill.
